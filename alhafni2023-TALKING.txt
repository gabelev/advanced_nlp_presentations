
SLIDE 2 — Why Arabic GEC is Hard
- Morphological richness is the dominant challenge. Arabic's inflectional system
  produces a vocabulary so large that out-of-vocabulary rates dwarf English,
  which means statistical pattern matching is harder and models need to
  generalize across morphological paradigms.
- The diglossia point is underappreciated: even educated native writers mix MSA
  and dialectal forms. The boundary between "error" and "register choice" is
  genuinely blurry, which makes annotation expensive and inconsistent.
- The 40% punctuation error rate is striking — and directly relevant to your
  work, since ERRANT's punctuation (PUNCT) category is a known noise source in
  round-trip MT pipelines. Arabic amplifies this problem by an order of magnitude.
- Data scarcity is the hard constraint that shapes every architectural decision
  in this paper. Training sets measured in tens of thousands of words force
  simpler models than English GEC would use.

SLIDE 3 — Three Contributions in One Paper
- Contribution 1 (Seq2Seq benchmark) fills an obvious gap: nobody had
  systematically evaluated pretrained Arabic Transformers on GEC before.
- Contribution 2 (multi-class GED + annotation pipeline) is the most
  infrastructural — they're building ERRANT for Arabic essentially from scratch.
- Contribution 3 (GED → GEC study) directly parallels Yuan et al. 2021 in
  English, making this paper a cross-lingual replication and extension of that
  work.
- For your paper: this is the closest analog to what you're doing in Russian,
  Ukrainian, and Slovene — applying GED-informed GEC methodology to an
  under-resourced non-English language. The parallel is explicit and citable.

SLIDE 4 — Three Arabic GEC Datasets
- The dataset split (L1 native vs. L2 learner) matters: L1 errors skew toward
  spelling and punctuation, L2 errors toward morphology and syntax. Systems
  trained on one don't transfer cleanly to the other.
- 25–30% erroneous words is high by GEC standards. English learner corpora
  typically run 5–15%. This increases recall sensitivity and means F0.5's
  precision weighting is even more important here.
- No manual error type annotations in any of the three datasets is the key
  gap that motivates the annotation pipeline on the next slide. The paper has
  to build its own supervision signal from scratch.
- ZAEBUC is the smallest (25K words) and has no prior published results,
  so any result here establishes the baseline for future work.

SLIDE 5 — Building an Arabic GED Pipeline
- The edit extraction problem is non-trivial for Arabic because merge and split
  operations (two words becoming one, or one word splitting into two) are
  common and break standard Levenshtein alignment.
- The new iterative greedy algorithm achieves AER 0.00 vs. 0.10–0.16 for
  prior methods — a clean, near-perfect result that justifies building the
  whole pipeline on top of it.
- ARETA is the Arabic equivalent of ERRANT for error type taxonomy, but it
  had its own alignment issues. Swapping in the new algorithm increases
  coverage meaningfully.
- The three granularity levels (2-class, 13-class, 43-class) mirror ERRANT's
  hierarchical structure and enable the same granularity ablation studies that
  Yuan et al. ran on English.

SLIDE 6 — GEC Models: Baselines
- Four baselines before the main models: Morph (rule-based morphological
  disambiguation), MLE (bigram lookup), ChatGPT (3-shot), and implicitly the
  pre-Transformer prior SOTA.
- ChatGPT's performance profile is instructive: highest recall but 38% of
  mismatches are wrong. GPT-3.5 is overcorrecting and hallucinating — a
  pattern consistent with its behavior on low-resource tasks generally.
- MLE has the highest precision of the baselines because it only corrects
  patterns seen in training. Useful as a conservative upper bound on precision.
- Morph needs no training data, which makes it valuable as a preprocessing step
  even when large pretrained models are available — it fills a different part
  of the error space.

SLIDE 7 — GEC Models: Seq2Seq with GED
- AraBART (24GB MSA, news-heavy) vs. AraT5 (256GB MSA + Twitter) — AraT5
  has 10x the pretraining data but AraBART ends up benefiting more from GED.
  Pretraining scale doesn't dominate here.
- GED integration is simpler than Yuan et al.: GED predictions become an
  additional token embedding summed before the encoder, rather than a full
  second encoder with gating. The simplification is motivated by data scarcity
  — adding parameters risks overfitting on 25–43K word training sets.
- Using predicted (not gold) GED tags during training is a deliberate choice
  to match inference-time noise. This is training-inference consistency and
  is the right call — otherwise the model learns to rely on cleaner labels
  than it will ever see at test time.
- This is directly relevant to your design: your joint head produces predicted
  tags that feed back into correction. The same training-inference consistency
  argument applies.

SLIDE 8 — Does Morphological Preprocessing Help?
- Yes, consistently — and the mechanism is clear: Morph improves recall by
  helping the model find and fix more errors, at a small precision cost.
- The QALB-2015-L2 exception is telling: the L2 learner error profile (more
  morphological and syntactic errors) doesn't align well with what a
  morphological disambiguator is designed to fix. Domain mismatch between
  the preprocessor and the error distribution limits the gains.
- Morph and GED are orthogonal interventions: Morph operates on the input
  before the model sees it, GED informs the encoder's representation. They
  address different failure modes and stack cleanly.
- For your pipeline: you don't have an equivalent morphological preprocessor
  for Russian/Ukrainian/Slovene, but the principle — that recall-improving
  preprocessing and error-type-aware encoding are complementary — is worth
  noting in your related work.

SLIDE 9 — Does GED Help Arabic GEC?
- Yes, mirroring the English result from Yuan et al. The GED finding
  generalizes across languages, which strengthens the case that it's a
  language-agnostic phenomenon rather than an English-specific quirk.
- AraBART benefits more than AraT5 from GED. Less pretraining data may mean
  the base model has a weaker prior on error patterns, so the explicit GED
  signal provides more new information.
- The gains are modest in absolute terms (79.1 → 79.3 on QALB-14) but
  consistent across all three datasets. Consistency across domains is the
  more important result than magnitude.
- Combining Morph + GED43 gives the best average results (75.3), confirming
  the complementarity finding from the previous slide.

SLIDE 10 — GED Granularity: No Clear Winner
- Unlike Yuan et al. (where 4-class won for fine-tuning), no single granularity
  consistently dominates here. 13-class edges out 43-class by 0.1 on average.
- The most important number on this slide is not the winner — it's the oracle
  gap. Oracle GED43 reaches 80.2 avg vs. 75.3 for predicted GED43. That's a
  ~5 point gap attributable entirely to GED accuracy, echoing the Yuan et al.
  oracle experiment.
- Cross-paper consistency of the oracle gap result is strong evidence that
  improving GED accuracy is the highest-leverage intervention in this class
  of systems — more valuable than architectural changes or granularity tuning.
- For your paper: the oracle gap result from both Yuan et al. and Alhafni et al.
  is a two-citation argument for why better detection (which your round-trip
  MT pivots improve) should translate to better correction.

SLIDE 11 — State-of-the-Art Results
- Prior SOTA on QALB-14 (Watson et al. 2018, F1=70.4) is pushed to 79.5 —
  a 9-point jump, which is large. Most of that comes from the pretrained
  Transformer, not the GED augmentation.
- QALB-2015-L2 improvement (73.2 → 77.1) is more modest but still meaningful
  given how small the training set is.
- ZAEBUC establishes a new benchmark with no prior comparison point, which
  means any future work on that dataset will cite this paper as the baseline.
  That's long-term citation value beyond just the SOTA claims.

SLIDE 12 — Summary
- Five takeaways that map cleanly onto the three contributions: new edit
  extraction algorithm, pretrained Seq2Seq models beat prior SOTA, GED
  generalizes to Arabic, Morph and GED are complementary, oracle gap
  identifies GED accuracy as the bottleneck.
- The cross-lingual generalization story is the most important thing for your
  paper: Alhafni et al. show that the Yuan et al. GED→GEC improvement pattern
  holds in Arabic. Your work extends this to Russian, Ukrainian, and Slovene —
  you're adding three more data points to a growing cross-lingual pattern.
- Cite this paper alongside Yuan et al. whenever you make the claim that
  multi-class GED improves GEC. Together they cover English + Arabic; your
  paper adds Slavic languages to that set.
