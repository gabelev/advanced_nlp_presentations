
SLIDE 2 — The Problem
- GEC is a supervised task: you need (ungrammatical, corrected) sentence pairs
  at scale. Real annotated data is expensive and limited.
- Prior synthetic approaches do things like randomly swap words or delete tokens.
  These produce errors that are too simple and too uniform.
- The downstream effect: a GEC model trained on that data learns shallow
  heuristics rather than genuine grammatical understanding.
- Bottom line: the quality and diversity of synthetic errors is the bottleneck,
  not the quantity.

SLIDE 3 — The Idea
- The fix is elegant: prepend an ERRANT error-type tag to the input before
  asking the model to corrupt it.
- The tag steers the model toward a specific error class (e.g., NOUN:INFL),
  so you get targeted, realistic corruptions instead of random noise.
- The example is intuitive — "sheeps" is exactly the kind of plausible,
  human-like mistake a learner would make, not an artifact of random deletion.
- ERRANT provides the tag vocabulary (25 categories), so the system is grounded
  in a well-established error taxonomy.

SLIDE 4 — What is ERRANT?
- ERRANT does token-level alignment between original and corrected sentences,
  then classifies each edit into one of 25 error types.
- It plays two distinct roles in this paper: (1) it generates the tagged
  training data for the corruption model, and (2) it's used at evaluation time
  to compute span-based F0.5 scores.
- F0.5 weights precision twice as heavily as recall — appropriate for GEC,
  where spurious corrections are worse than missed ones.
- Knowing the tag taxonomy helps you understand what "diversity" means here:
  it's not just surface variation, it's coverage across linguistically
  meaningful error categories.

SLIDE 5 — Two Corruption Model Variants
- Two architectures are explored: a full-sequence Transformer (tag on input)
  and Seq2Edits (tag on output, edit-level predictions).
- Seq2Edits is the better fit because error type prediction is native to its
  output format — the model is already thinking in terms of edits.
- The full-sequence model has to implicitly encode the error type in the
  generated surface form, which is a harder learning problem.
- This architectural choice matters for the FST constrained decoding discussed
  on the next slide.

SLIDE 6 — Constraining Seq2Edits with FSTs
- FSTs let you hard-constrain beam search to produce a specific tag — useful
  in theory for enforcing tag coverage during data generation.
- Three FST variants are tested with different permissiveness around the target
  tag (NoSigma, PostSigma, PrePostSigma).
- PostSigma is motivated by the "garden-path problem": beam search can commit
  to an incompatible token early, making the target tag unreachable later.
  PostSigma fixes this by allowing the target tag to fire at any point, then
  permitting free generation afterward.
- Punchline: constrained decoding fails at scale. The final system just uses
  unconstrained tagged Seq2Edits. The tag is enough signal on its own.

SLIDE 7 — Matching a Target Tag Distribution
- You have millions of sentences and 25 tags. How do you assign tags so the
  resulting corpus matches a desired error-type distribution?
- Three methods: Offline-Optimal (min-cost flow, maximizes model confidence),
  Offline-Probabilistic (sample tag, find matching sentences), Online (sample
  tag independently per sentence, O(N)).
- Offline-Optimal is the theoretical optimum but paradoxically performs worst.
  Why? Because maximizing model confidence collapses diversity — every
  VERB:SVA corruption looks the same.
- Online sampling scales to 200M sentences and performs best. This foreshadows
  the broader sampling-vs-beam-search theme.

SLIDE 8 — Tagged vs. Untagged: BEA-dev F0.5
- Anchor points: seed model (no fine-tuning) = 33.7, real parallel data = 50.4.
  That's the ceiling we're trying to approach with synthetic data.
- Untagged back-translation gets to 42.4 — a solid baseline but still well
  below real data.
- Tagged Seq2Edits + Online reaches 48.0 — closing most of the gap with no
  real parallel source sentences.
- The jump from 42.4 → 48.0 is entirely attributable to the tagging mechanism.
  That's the paper's core empirical result.

SLIDE 9 — Adapting to Native English
- Native and non-native writers make structurally different errors: natives
  produce more PUNCT and SPELL errors; non-natives produce more DET errors.
- By simply re-weighting the tag distribution to match native error patterns,
  the synthetic corpus adapts to that domain.
- Result: native-distribution synthetic data (42.9 F0.5) actually beats real
  parallel data (42.1 F0.5) on the native test set.
- This is a strong demonstration of the framework's flexibility — tag
  distributions are a clean knob for domain adaptation without any new data.

SLIDE 10 — What is Beam Search? (Primer)
- Background slide for audience members unfamiliar with sequence decoding.
- Beam search keeps the top-k partial sequences at each step. k=1 is greedy;
  larger k explores more but is still mode-seeking.
- Key property: beam search always finds the highest-probability output, not a
  sample from the distribution. That's fine for translation, problematic for
  diverse data generation.
- Sets up the next slide's contrast between Stahlberg & Kumar and SeLex-RT.

SLIDE 11 — Beam Search: Two Different Roles
- This slide draws a direct contrast with SeLex-RT, which is relevant to your
  own multilingual GEC work.
- In Stahlberg & Kumar, beam search collapses diversity: every corruption of
  the same type converges to the same surface form. Sampling fixes this.
- In SeLex-RT, diversity comes from the pivot language matrix (bg/en/zh), not
  the decoder. Within each pivot, you want clean, deterministic translation,
  so beam search is appropriate.
- Takeaway: the right decoding strategy depends on where diversity is supposed
  to come from in your pipeline.

SLIDE 12 — Sampling Beats Beam Search
- Empirical confirmation: sampling during corruption generation improves BEA-dev
  F0.5 from 52.9 to 54.7 — a meaningful gain from a single decoding change.
- Intuition: beam search produces the most predictable error, which lets the
  GEC model learn a surface shortcut rather than the underlying phenomenon.
  Sampling forces the model to generalize.
- This mirrors the back-translation literature in MT (Edunov et al., 2018),
  where noisy/sampled back-translation consistently beats beam search output.
- Practical implication for your pipeline: if you're generating synthetic
  corruptions with mBART, consider sampling over beam search.

SLIDE 13 — C4200M and State of the Art
- Scaling the tagged corruption pipeline to 200M C4 sentences produces a
  pre-training corpus that sets SOTA at the time on both BEA-test (74.9)
  and CoNLL-14 (68.3).
- Previous SOTA (Omelianchuk et al.) used GECToR, a specialized edit-tagging
  architecture. This work matches and exceeds it with vanilla Transformers.
- All gains are from data, not architecture. That's the paper's strongest
  claim: with good synthetic data, you don't need model complexity.
- For your multilingual work: the C4200M result suggests this approach is
  scalable — the bottleneck is data quality/diversity, not model size.

SLIDE 14 — Summary
- Five clean takeaways: (1) error type tags → more realistic/diverse corruptions;
  (2) tag distribution matching enables domain adaptation; (3) native-distribution
  synthetic data beats real data on native English; (4) sampling beats beam
  search; (5) C4200M sets SOTA with no architecture changes.
- The through-line: structured error taxonomy (ERRANT) + diversity-preserving
  decoding (sampling) + flexible tag distribution matching = a powerful,
  domain-adaptable synthetic data pipeline.
- For your GEC research: this paper is your baseline corruption strategy
  (Stahlberg & Kumar synthetic corruption), and slides 11-12 directly justify
  your design choices in the SeLex-RT extension.
